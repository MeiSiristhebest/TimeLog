Abstract
While voice interfaces promise natural interaction for older adults, current cloud-dependent
solutionsfailpreciselywheretheyareneededmost: inreal-worldenvironmentswithunstable
connectivity. TimeLogisamobileappthatseparateslocalrecording(whichalwaysworks)
fromcloud-basedAIfeatures(whichrunonlywhentheinternetisstable). Whenthenetwork
drops,theappprovidesaclearlocalaudiocuetoreassuretheuserthatitisstilllistening.
TheMVPsupportsthreecorefeatures: (i)GuidedInterviewwithoptionalfollow-upprompts,
(ii)anoffline-capableStoryGalleryforreplayingrecordings,and(iii)alocal“TopicLibrary”
containing pre-seeded and family-submitted interview questions that guide the user without
needingareal-timecall.
We describe the system design and outline an exploratory within-subjects evaluation (10–
15 participants) against a standard voice recorder. The primary goal is to verify recording
survivability(whetherthefileissavedlocally)andtogatherpreliminaryusabilityfeedback.
Secondary measures include usability (System Usability Scale) and perceived workload
(NASA-TLX). This work contributes (1) a reference architecture for fault-tolerant voice
interfaces,and(2)initialevidenceontheefficacyoflocal-firstfallbacksinreducingcognitive
loadforolderadults.
Keywords: Older Adults, Voice Interaction, Offline-First, Local-First Architecture, Smart
Recording,Resilience.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1 Background and Motivation
Theglobalpopulationisagingrapidly. Adultsaged65andabovenowrepresentanincreas-
inglyactivesegmentofsmartphoneusers. However,age-relatedchanges—suchasdeclining
vision, reduced hand steadiness, slower thinking speed, and shorter memory span—make
standard smartphone apps difficult to use [1], [2], [3]. Research reports that older adults
complete fewer tasks and feel more anxious when using typical mobile apps, especially for
creativeactivitieslikewritingorrecording[3],[4],[5].
Beyond these physical challenges, older adults have a strong psychological need to share
their life stories. According to psychologist Erik Erikson, seniors seek “Generativity”—a
desiretoleavealegacyandpasswisdomtoyoungergenerations[6],[7]. Priorworkreports
associationsbetweenstructuredreminiscenceactivitiesandimprovedwellbeingoutcomesin
olderadults[8],[9]. Thisnaturaldesiretosharestories,combinedwithitspotentialbenefits,
suggeststhatmanyolderadultswouldusedigitalstorytellingtools—ifonlythesetoolswere
easier to use. Yet, current tools often impose interaction complexity that can limit adoption
andsustaineduse.
Interface-leveladaptations—suchaslargertextorhighercontrastdisplays—helpwithvision
problems but do not fully address interaction complexity: apps with too many menus and
stepsexhaustolderusersmentally[1],[10],[11],[12]. Asaresult,manyseniorsgiveupon
creativedigitalactivitiesentirely[13].
Voice assistants like Siri and Alexa reduce typing demands, but they still require users to
giveprecisecommands[14]. Thesesystemsalsocannotrememberpastconversationsorask
helpful follow-up questions—features that are essential for natural storytelling [14], [15].
Most importantly, few current mobile apps combine natural conversation with the ability to
keepworkingwheninternetconnectionsarepoor—acommonprobleminreal-worlduse.
This project addresses this gap by developing Time Log, a voice-first mobile application
for low-friction story recording. The system targets one-tap initiation and reliable local
persistence, so that older adults can continue recording even when internet connections are
unstable.
1.2 Problem Statement
Despitehighsmartphoneownership,olderadultsfacethreespecificbarrierswhenrecording
lifestories:
1

1. Cognitive Overload: Multi-step menus in standard apps strain working memory,
leadingtoearlyabandonment[3],[4],[10].
2. LackofScaffolding: Passivevoicerecordersdonotprovideprompts,oftenresulting
in“writer’sblock”orsilence[14],[15],[16].
3. Network Fragility: Most voice agents depend on constant connectivity. Network
latency or outages disrupt the conversational flow and can lead to data loss, causing

Time Log addresses these gaps by prioritizing a “record-first, process-later” workflow. The
system captures audio locally to prevent loss, reconciling with cloud services only when
connectionstabilityreturns.
MVP Definition: The MVP integrates three Must-Have features (Guided Interview, Story
Gallery, Topic Library) and the local-first infrastructure described in Section 5.3. The
system preserves local recording and replay even under complete connectivity loss. Online
AI prompting is an optional enhancement enabled only when connection quality meets
predefinedthresholds.

2.1.1 TheDigitalDivideandPsychologicalBarriers
Smartphone adoption among adults aged 65+ has steadily increased. However, research
showsthata“second-level”digitaldividepersists—definednotbyaccessbutbydisparities
in functional and cognitive usage. This gap blocks digital participation in later life. Large-
scale analyses have demonstrated strong associations between low digital competence and
reducedsocialengagement,heightenedpsychologicaldistress,andpoorerhealthtrajectories
amongolderadults[2],[18].
These inequalities are not solely technological but cognitive in nature. Age-related declines
inworkingmemory,attentionalcontrol,andprocessingspeedmakeconventionalmulti-step
interfaces disproportionately difficult to navigate. Studies on technology-related anxiety
indicate that procedural complexity and unfamiliar interaction patterns significantly reduce
adoption among older users [3]. Rural living environments and limited informal digital
support worsen digital exclusion and contribute to social isolation [11]. Functional digital
inequality is thus a multidimensional challenge shaped by cognitive, emotional, and envi-
ronmentalconstraints.
2.1.2 HCIDesignPrinciplesforAgingPopulations
Human–computerinteraction(HCI)researchoverthepasttwodecadeshasproducedwidely
adoptedgerontechnologyguidelinesaimedatreducingperceptualandmotorbarriers. These
includeenlargedtypefaces,high-contrastcolourpalettes,simplifiedvisualhierarchies,min-
imisation of fine-grained gestures, and a general emphasis on recognition rather than recall
[1], [4], [5], [10]. Such recommendations have been shown to improve basic usability and
immediatetaskcomprehension.
However, perceptual optimisation alone is insufficient. Older users often struggle less
with the presentation layer than with deeper architectural features—such as hierarchical
navigation, mode switching, fragmented interaction flows, and tasks requiring explicit file
management[4],[10]. Studiesonanxietyandresistancetochangerevealthatwheninterfaces
requiresequentialdecision-makingorunstructuredinput,drop-offincreasessubstantially[3],
7

[13]. Thishighlightsastructurallimitation: standardinterfacedesignswerenotdesignedfor
thecognitiveageingprofile.
2.1.3 GenerativeAIasaProactiveAssistant
Advancesinlargelanguagemodels(LLMs)haveenabledashiftfromrigid,command-based
tools to conversational agents capable of sustaining context, interpreting ambiguous input,
and adapting responses to user preferences [14], [17]. In the context of ageing populations,
recent studies report high acceptability of AI-powered companions for tasks such as daily
check-ins, medication reminders, emotional support, and general wellbeing management
[15],[20]. Systemsdesignedspecificallyforolderadults—suchasspecializedchatbotsused
inhealthcareandelderlycareenvironments—demonstrateimprovedengagementrelativeto
traditionalvoiceassistants. Thisisparticularlytrueamonguserswithlimiteddigitalliteracy
[20],[21].
Despitetheseencouragingfindings,threenotablegapsremaininexistingdeployments:
• Limited focus on autobiographical memory and narrative preservation: Current
systems prioritise health monitoring or informational tasks, with little emphasis on
long-termmemorycaptureorintergenerationalcommunication[15],[22].
• Lack of guided prompting with offline fallback: While LLM chatbots maintain
context,publiclydocumentedsystemsrarelysupportprolongedreminiscencesessions
withoptionalscaffoldingthatcontinuesworkingunderintermittentconnectivity[14],
[17].
• Fragility under network instability: Existing AI companions typically rely on
continuous,high-bandwidthcloudconnectivity. Inruralorinstitutionalenvironments
withintermittentconnectivity,thesesystemsmayfailentirelyratherthanswitchingto
alocalrecordingmodetopreservetheuser’snarrative[17].
These limitations show that current generative systems, while technically advanced, remain
unsuitedforfullyautonomous,privacy-sensitive,narrative-centeredusecases.

Chapter 6. Discussion and Conclusion
6.1 Limitations
While the proposed system introduces a hybrid architecture for accessible narrative preser-
vation,ithasseverallimitations.
6.1.1 TechnicalLimitations
• Third-Party API Dependency: The cloud-based intelligence layer relies on exter-
nal providers (Deepgram for STT, Google Gemini for LLM inference, LiveKit for
WebRTC).ChangesinAPIpricing,termsofservice,orunexpecteddeprecationcould
impactsystemviability. Themodulararchitectureisdesignedtoreduceswitchingcost,
thoughprovidersubstitutionwouldstillrequirere-validationoflatencyandquality.
• Latency Variability: The system targets sub-second response times under typical
connectivity; under severely degraded networks, Time Log disables live prompting
andpreservescaptureviaofflinemode.
• Speech Recognition Accuracy: Automatic speech recognition (ASR) performance
varies with regional accents, dialects, and non-standard speech patterns common
among older adults. While the system supports traditional listening with human
post-processing,real-timetranscriptaccuracymayrequireiterativetuningforspecific
linguisticcommunities.
6.1.2 StudyLimitations
• Sample Size and Demographics: The planned evaluation involves 10–15 partici-
pants, which limits generalizability across diverse cultural and linguistic populations.
This sample size is appropriate for exploratory usability research within a graduation
projectscope.
• AccountRecoveryAssumption: Reliablerecoverymaydependontheavailabilityof
atrustedfamilycontact;thisassumptionmaynotholdforsociallyisolatedusers.
• Geographic and Cultural Context: The primary user research and persona devel-
opment were conducted within a Chinese urban context. User motivations, family
dynamics,andtechnologyattitudesmaydiffersignificantlyinotherculturalsettings.
• Short-TermEvaluation: Theproposedstudymeasuresimmediateusabilityandtask
completion. Long-term adoption, sustained engagement, and the wellbeing effects of
regularuseremainunvalidatedwithintheprojectscope.
40

• Non-Therapeutic Scope: Time Log is designed for storytelling, not as a therapeutic
or medical intervention. While prior research suggests associations between reminis-
cence activities and wellbeing, therapeutic outcomes are outside the validation scope
ofthisMVP.
6.1.3 TechnologyPortability
Thesystemisdesignedwithswappablecomponentstoreducevendorlock-in:
• Speech Services: Currently Deepgram; can be replaced with Google Cloud Speech,
Azure,oropen-sourceWhisperifpricingoravailabilitychanges.
• LLM:CurrentlyGoogleGemini;canbereplacedwithOpenAI,AnthropicClaude,or
localmodels.
• Backend: Currently Supabase; can be migrated to Firebase or self-hosted Post-
greSQL.
Thismodularityreducesvendorlock-inandallowsadaptationtofuturetechnologychanges.
6.2 Future Work
Weidentifyseveraldirectionsforfuturedevelopment:
1. FamilyDashboard: Aweb-basedinterfaceforfamilymemberstoreviewrecordings,
submit questions, and manage shared content. The current architecture already sup-
portsfamilyauthenticationanddevicepairing;extendingthistoadedicateddashboard
isanaturalnextstep.
2. Animated Agent Feedback: A vector-animated avatar (e.g., an Owl companion) to
providevisualstatecues—listening,thinking,speaking—reducingrelianceontextand
buildingemotionalconnectionwithusers.
3. RAG-Based Personalization: Future versions could use Retrieval-Augmented Gen-
eration (RAG) to tailor prompts based on previous recordings (e.g., “Last week you
mentioned your grandmother. Tell me more about her”). The current local-first
architecture already stores transcripts locally, providing a foundation for this feature
withoutrefactoring.
4. Real-Time Family Chat: Expanding the current pre-seeded “Topic Library” into
a live bidirectional channel where family members could send questions dynami-
cally and receive audio responses in real-time. The existing WebRTC infrastructure
(LiveKit)canbeextendedtosupportthis.
41

5. Multimodal Elicitation: Integrating Computer Vision to allow users to photograph
olditemsandgeneratecontext-specificquestions.
6. Longitudinal Studies: Conducting long-term deployment to measure the wellbeing
impactofreminiscence.
6.3 Conclusion
This project proposes and prototypes Time Log, a voice-first application designed to make
digital storytelling accessible to older adults. By decoupling the recording interface from
networkdependency,TimeLogmitigatestheriskofstorylossunderpoorconnectivity. The
planned exploratory evaluation (10–15 participants) will test whether local-first persistence
improvesrecordingsurvivability(H1),whetheroptionalpromptsreduceperceivedworkload
(H2), and whether the Sound Cue lowers anxiety during network failures (H3). Beyond
technical resilience, the system embraces a user-centric philosophy: it adapts technology
to the senior, rather than forcing the senior to adapt to the technology. It is intended to
support needs related to legacy, connection, and independence. Time Log is designed to
lower technical barriers to preserving family memories—helping older adults share their
stories,onememoryatatime.
